E-commerce thrives on the gap between _displayed savings_ and _actual savings_. A "70% off + 20% extra" sticker is not 90% off — it's 76%. A "MRP ₹2,499, deal price ₹999" is not 60% off — once you remove GST from both sides, it's closer to 55%. The discount calculator handles all of this.

## Stacked discounts: the multiplication rule

Stacked percentage discounts **multiply**, they don't add. Two discounts of `a%` and `b%` together produce a final discount of:

`combined = 1 − (1 − a/100) × (1 − b/100)`

| Sticker math     | Actual math           |
| ---------------- | --------------------- |
| 70% + 20% = 90%  | 1 − 0.30 × 0.80 = 76% |
| 50% + 50% = 100% | 1 − 0.50 × 0.50 = 75% |
| 30% + 10% = 40%  | 1 − 0.70 × 0.90 = 37% |

The bigger the stack, the bigger the gap. The "100% off" version is the classic giveaway that the math isn't additive — if it were, two 50% discounts would make everything free.

## The four modes the calculator supports

1. **Find the discount** — original price → discounted price → what % savings is that?
2. **Find the final price** — original price → discount % → what do I pay?
3. **Find the original price** — final price → discount % → what was the pre-sale price? (Useful for spotting fake discounts.)
4. **Stacked discounts** — original price + list of discount %s → final price + effective single %

## The MRP-vs-deal-price trick

In India, MRP is _maximum_ retail price. Many products carry an MRP printed at 2–3× the realistic retail price specifically to make every "deal" look like a steep discount. The fix: compare with the price _across multiple sellers_, not against the MRP.

## When the discount is real and worth taking

- **Year-end sales** (Dec–Jan, Mar): genuine inventory clearance, deepest cuts
- **Festive (Big Billion / Great Indian)**: usually real on electronics, often fake on apparel
- **Coupon-stack windows** during launches: bank offer + card offer + first-purchase code can stack to real 30–40% off on premium items
