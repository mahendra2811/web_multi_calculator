## What is a volume converter?

A **volume converter** translates between units of capacity — liters, milliliters, cubic meters, gallons (US and UK), cups, tablespoons, teaspoons. Volume conversions matter in cooking, fuel, chemistry, plumbing, and shipping.

The trap: **US and UK gallons are different**, and cup sizes vary by country. Cooking recipes from different countries can be silently wrong if you assume one cup is universal.

## Worked example

A US recipe calls for **2 cups of milk** (US cups). You're in India with metric cups (250 mL):

```
2 US cups × 236.59 mL = 473.18 mL
1 metric cup = 250 mL
473.18 / 250 = 1.89 metric cups
```

So fill your metric measuring cup to **about 1¾ cups (1¾ × 250 = 437 mL is slightly less; 1⅞ ≈ 470 mL is closer)**. Or use the mL number directly: 473 mL.

## US vs UK Gallons — important difference

| Unit                 | Liters |
| -------------------- | ------ |
| US gallon            | 3.785  |
| UK (Imperial) gallon | 4.546  |

UK gallon is **20% bigger** than US gallon. Important for:

- Vehicle fuel economy (UK MPG is 20% higher than US MPG for the same car)
- Industrial chemical orders quoted in gallons
- Recipe books from different eras / regions

## Cooking cups — the worst confusion

| Cup variant                        | mL                   |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| US Customary Cup                   | 236.59               |
| US "Legal" Cup                     | 240 (FDA labeling)   |
| Metric Cup (Australia, NZ, Canada) | 250                  |
| UK Cup                             | 284.13 (rarely used) |

US recipes use US Customary (≈ 237 mL). Most Indian / Australian / Asian recipes use metric (250 mL). 5% difference doesn't matter for stews; matters a lot for cakes.

For baking precision: **weigh ingredients in grams** instead of measuring by volume. Volume is unreliable; weight is exact.

## Considerations

- **Cubic vs liquid units.** 1 cubic centimeter (cc) = 1 mL exactly. 1 m³ = 1,000 L exactly.
- **Wet vs dry measure.** Cups in baking are typically dry-measure (filled level). Wet measure is checked at eye level. The math is identical; only the technique differs.
- **Tbsp vs tsp ratio is universal.** 1 tbsp = 3 tsp = 15 mL = ½ fl oz. Same across countries.
- **Australian tbsp = 20 mL**, not 15. Australian and NZ recipes are exception cases.

## Limitations

- The calculator doesn't auto-detect US vs metric cups — pick which cup type from the dropdown.
- Doesn't handle barrels of oil (different categories — see Industrial Units).
- Doesn't model temperature-dependent volume changes (water expands slightly at higher temperatures).
